# Choose a result, then follow the recipe

Playbooks turn a useful idea into something you can actually complete. Each one tells you **what to give the AI, what to copy, what to review, and what you should have when you finish**.

You do not need to understand automation before starting. Begin with chat, graduate to an agent workspace, and automate only after the manual version works reliably.

## Which setup should I use?

You can use the same playbook in four different ways.

| Setup | Best when | What you do |
|---|---|---|
| **Normal AI chat** | You want the fastest first attempt | Paste the quick-start prompt and your source material. Save the result yourself. |
| **Folder-based agent** | You want repeatable files and clear inputs/outputs | Create the suggested folder, start the agent there, and let it read and write only inside that workspace. |
| **Obsidian vault** | The output should become maintained knowledge | Point the agent at a dedicated vault folder and require links, properties, and review dates. |
| **Connected automation** | The manual version has already succeeded several times | Add a trigger, validation, approval, logging, and a failure path. |

!!! tip "New here?"
    Use **normal AI chat** for your first run. Use a **folder-based agent** for your second or third run when you know which files and outputs you want.

## What “follow the playbook” means

A playbook is a reusable process. You keep the process and replace the example input.

For example, the research playbook can investigate:

- whether your team should adopt a tool;
- a company before a sales meeting;
- a technology you are learning;
- a comparison between several products;
- a developing topic that needs current sources.

The workflow stays the same:

<div class="playbook-flow">
  <span>Input</span><b>→</b><span>Plan</span><b>→</b><span>Collect</span><b>→</b><span>Draft</span><b>→</b><span>Review</span><b>→</b><span>Final result</span>
</div>

The pages include a **quick version** for immediate use and a **full version** for repeatable work.

## The human is still part of the system

Every playbook stops at a reviewable draft unless it explicitly says otherwise. The AI may organize, compare, extract, and draft. A person should still approve:

- factual or source-sensitive claims;
- decisions and action ownership;
- private or sensitive information;
- external messages;
- publishing, sending, deleting, purchasing, or changing shared systems.

!!! warning "A polished answer is not proof"
    Review the source support, dates, names, links, assumptions, and requested action before treating an output as final.

## A simple way to run any playbook

1. Open the playbook that matches your goal.
2. Read **What you will make** and **What you need**.
3. Choose the **chat**, **agent workspace**, or **Obsidian** path.
4. Copy the quick-start prompt.
5. Replace every item in `[square brackets]`.
6. Ask the AI to show its plan before doing broad work.
7. Review the intermediate evidence or extraction.
8. Let it draft the final result.
9. Complete the human checklist.
10. Save the approved output and record corrections for next time.

## When to automate

Do not connect a trigger just because a tool makes it easy. Move through these stages:

1. **Manual:** you follow the page in chat.
2. **Supervised agent:** the agent works inside a bounded folder and you approve its plan and output.
3. **Structured workflow:** inputs, outputs, checks, errors, and logs are defined.
4. **Connected automation:** a trigger starts low-risk steps and approval protects high-impact actions.
5. **Monitored operation:** failures, costs, corrections, and stale instructions are reviewed over time.

Start with one successful result. Then make it repeatable.
